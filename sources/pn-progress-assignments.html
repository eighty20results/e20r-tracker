December 9th, 2014 2014/12/09 Dear Newbie...

Imagine a newbie, just like you, starting PN Coaching. Give them THREE pieces of advice from your own experience.

Start by ignoring everything you think you know, every fitness and health article or blog post you've ever read, and be ready to observe, listen, and learn. Not about what to eat (or not to eat), how to burn more calories, avoid food X, or what to do in the gym. This isn't about the number on the scale. It's not even about how you look or wind up looking. This is about learning to see yourself for who and what you are; a unique human being with unique challenges, unique needs and unique expectations. But before you can truly value your uniqueness as part of your healthy and fit self, you probably need to "reset" your expectations and mindset. Let it happen. It's going to pay off.

The most important lesson I've taken away from this program? There are essentially 3 options available to us for every decision:

1: take a "positive" action related to the decision;
2: take a "negative" action related to the decision, or;
3: take no action related to the decision.

The first two means you're taking ownership and control. The last one means you're giving control away to someone that isn't you. In my world, the last one is the least human of the 3 experiences. It's also, I think, the fastest path back to your old (or current) self.

Sometimes there's a lesson that will trip you up. It'll trip you up for what seems like no good reason at all. Let it. Maybe you're not ready to deal with what the lesson is making you think about. Maybe you can't "see it" for the life of you. Sometimes, it's because it's a non-issue for you. Sometimes it's something you're not ready to deal with at all. And sometimes, it's so close to you, you can't possibly expect to see it all within in a day. Those last two "somethings"? Those are the best! Just decide that you're not ready to deal with it right now. Put it away, then write yourself a "TODO" to go back and revisit it later (tomorrow or next week). At that point, the lesson will probably seem a lot more manageable.

What have you tried in the past to lose weight?

The first (and only) diet I (we) ever tried was the Zone diet

Up to 3 days of "Green Faces" per instance (if it's green - i.e a green vegetable - or it had/will have a face, I could eat it) - It's kind of a hard-core Paleo like thing, before "Paleo" became the "in thing" to do.

How did that work for you in the long term?

The Zone diet, being that it was a diet, was a total bust. We were on the diet for a handful of days. No clue if I lost any weight since I was in my "no scale shall pass this doorway" phase at that time.

Green Faces was always a 1-3 day per instance type of thing and it seemed to help on the scale (and I am not aware of gaining the weight back). Keeping in mind that it was part of an overall multi-year effort to lose about 60 lbs (and keep 'em off for the better part of a decade so far). I viewed the stints as a short-term "kick my metabolism in gear" so although it was normally incredibly difficult to complete the days, my ability to focus for the short term and override a lot of "normal" behavior when I do, allowed me to do at least a handful of these over the span of about 6-12 months.

Paleo was something we tried as an experiment. We liked how we felt and when, after the "prescribed" 30 days (In Robb Wolf's book) we tried to reintroduce grain, dairy and sugar it left a sour "taste". As a result we stayed Paleo for about 2 years. Not sure why we gave it up to be honest. It worked pretty well from a weight loss and energy management perspective. I went from ~175lbs to ~159/160 in a couple of weeks and kept it off for the full 2 years. I also completed a couple of Marathons, a number of 1/2 marathons and a Goofy Challenge (1/2 Marathon on Saturday followed by a full Marathon on Sunday). My conclusion at the time (went "off the wagon" last year) was that my body (and palate) _really_ enjoyed the local meats/vegetables/fruits and I was able to perform very well athletically.

I think the issue at the time was the Goofy Challenge. Although it was in FL, the temp during the two days was between 90-95F with 87-97% humidity so by the time I was done running, I genuinely felt like I was done running, permanently. I was burnt out. As a consequence, it became really difficult to stay off of bread - having grown up in a European culture (Norwegian) where bread is the primary ingredient for breakfast, lunch and the evening snack. Once I had a slice of bread, it was pretty much over. The 'simple starch' addictions returned full force.

The 21 day Vegan Jump Start was something I knew wasn't going to last. I want my animals to be happy before I eat them - grass fed, local and pasture raised - but I'm not seeing myself giving up meats anytime soon. We started the experiment to be better informed about what a plant-based diet does to our individual athletic performance & strength. The result was pretty abysmal for us. Couple that with the fact that eating a case of supermarket bananas in a week is more than even the most devoted primate would be likely to consume, it was a doomed experiment from about day 2. I lasted a full 2 weeks, but by then my workouts were suffering so badly I decided to call it. I was having a rough enough time with my lack of desire to go for a run, I didn't need to feel crappy during my resistance training as well!

It's not really a matter of doing the opposite for me, I think... It's more about tweaking what I know works to make it less of a sacrifice to maintain it. But, that said, and in the spirit of the exercise:

I'm not going to do something as extreme as "The Goofy Challenge" ever again. The degree to which I "burned out" from those two days, in spite of having had fun with friends in Disney for a long weekend, was totally unexpected and although it's been more than a year at this point, I've been unable to "get myself outside for a run" with any regularity. I will _never_ go on a diet again. Period. Although I made that decision roughly 10 years ago, it still holds true.

I will not follow any single - semi-cultish - "this is the only way to eat" based philosophy. Its too limiting and leaves too big of a possibility for a harsh 'snap-back'.
